The Great Gatsby

"The Great Gatsby" by F. Scott Fitzgerald is a classic American novel that explores themes of wealth, love, and the American Dream in the Roaring Twenties. The story is narrated by Nick Carraway, a young man who moves to Long Island and becomes entangled in the lives of his wealthy and enigmatic neighbor Jay Gatsby and his cousin Daisy Buchanan. The novel paints a vivid portrait of the excesses and superficiality of the Jazz Age, where extravagant parties and opulent lifestyles mask deeper desires and disillusionments. Gatsby, in particular, is driven by his unrequited love for Daisy and his pursuit of the elusive American Dream. "The Great Gatsby" is a powerful commentary on the hollowness of materialism and the complexities of human aspirations. It remains a poignant and relevant work in American literature.

About the Author

F. Scott Fitzgerald was an American novelist and short story writer known for his portrayal of the Jazz Age. He was born in 1896 and is considered one of the greatest American writers of the 20th century.

Fitzgerald's works often explore the themes of wealth, decadence, and the American Dream. His writing style is celebrated for its lyricism and keen social commentary.
