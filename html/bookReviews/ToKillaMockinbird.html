To Kill a Mockingbird

"To Kill a Mockingbird" by Harper Lee is a classic American novel that addresses issues of race, class, and morality in the Deep South during the 1930s. The story is narrated by Scout Finch, a young girl growing up in the racially segregated town of Maycomb, Alabama. The novel revolves around the trial of Tom Robinson, a Black man falsely accused of raping a white woman. Scout's father, Atticus Finch, serves as Tom Robinson's defense attorney, and the trial becomes a focal point for the town's racial tensions and prejudices. "To Kill a Mockingbird" is a poignant exploration of injustice and the loss of innocence, as Scout and her brother Jem come to terms with the harsh realities of the adult world. It is a powerful story that continues to resonate with readers and is considered a classic of American literature.

About the Author

Harper Lee was an American novelist best known for her groundbreaking work, "To Kill a Mockingbird." She was born in Monroeville, Alabama, in 1926.

Lee's debut novel, "To Kill a Mockingbird," won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction in 1961 and became an instant classic. Her storytelling addressed complex social issues and is celebrated for its portrayal of moral growth and the loss of innocence.
